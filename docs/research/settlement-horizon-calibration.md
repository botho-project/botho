# Calibrating `SETTLEMENT_HORIZON_BLOCKS`

**Issue:** [#833](https://github.com/botho-project/botho/issues/833) (blocks [#831](https://github.com/botho-project/botho/issues/831); shares the horizon with [#925](https://github.com/botho-project/botho/issues/925))
**Status:** Simulation + recommendation. The final constant is ratified by the maintainer at review, then landed by #831 / #925 (ADR 0003).
**Reproduce:**
```
cargo run -p bth-cluster-tax --features cli --bin cluster-tax-sim -- settlement-horizon-sweep
```
Sim source: `cluster-tax/src/simulation/settlement_horizon_sweep.rs` (deterministic; seed `0xB07A_0833`). Every number below is regenerated by that command — nothing is hand-computed.

---

## 1. What the parameter is

ADR 0003 makes **factor-1 (background) coins the only wrappable class** and adds a
**demurrage-settlement operation** (#831) so a holder of a wealthy coin can *pay* to
reclassify it down to factor-1 and become wrap-eligible. The price is a **capitalized
future demurrage** over a fixed horizon:

```
settlement_charge = demurrage_charge(value, factor, SETTLEMENT_HORIZON_BLOCKS,
                                     rate_bps, blocks_per_year)
```

`SETTLEMENT_HORIZON_BLOCKS` is a **monetary-policy magnitude** — how much a wealthy coin
pays to permanently exit demurrage by wrapping. The two failure modes (issue body):

- **Too low** → wrapping is a cheap demurrage escape; wealthy holders wrap to dodge
  redistribution, eroding the cluster-tilted Gini gain the whole mechanism exists to
  produce.
- **Too high** → wrapping wealthy value is punitively expensive; the bridge on-ramp is
  unusable for exactly the holders who most want DeFi liquidity.

## 2. The structural fact that anchors everything

`demurrage_charge` (`cluster-tax/src/demurrage.rs`) is **linear in `elapsed`** for fixed
value, factor and rate. Therefore:

```
settlement_charge(H) ≡ H years-worth of ordinary demurrage, paid up front.
```

So `SETTLEMENT_HORIZON_BLOCKS`, expressed in years, **is** the holding period at which
settling-and-wrapping breaks even against holding-and-paying ordinary demurrage. This gives
the #831 "no strictly-dominant escape" soundness gate an analytic anchor, which the sim
confirms numerically: the **churn-invariance margin** (settlement charge ÷ H-years of
ordinary demurrage) is exactly `1.0000` at every horizon × factor class (integer rounding
can only ever push it `>= 1.0`, never below). **Settlement is never cheaper than holding
over the horizon**, at any horizon. The soundness gate is satisfied structurally, so the
calibration reduces to the economic trade-off between the two failure modes.

## 3. Method

A focused, deterministic Monte-Carlo (same shape as the shipped
`decoy_quantile_sweep`), reusing the consensus kernel `demurrage_charge`, the real
`ClusterFactorCurve` to derive factor classes from cluster wealth, and the shared
`simulation::metrics::calculate_gini` — no second Gini or charge implementation.

- **Factor classes** span the top decile, each factor taken from the production
  log-domain sigmoid curve (`W_MID` = 100k BTH, saturating at 6x).
- **Wrap-cost table**: settlement charge as % of settled value + churn-invariance margin,
  per horizon × class.
- **dGini-erosion experiment**: 90 background holders (1x) + 10 wealthy holders (the top
  decile, 5.745x whale class), run 10 years. Compare two worlds:
  - **HOLD** — the top decile holds and pays ongoing demurrage (funding per-capita
    redistribution) the whole run.
  - **ESCAPE** — the top decile settles+wraps at year 0: it pays the one-shot settlement
    charge into the lottery pool, then its wrapped value leaves the
    demurrage/redistribution system (wBTH pays no further demurrage, ADR 0003 §4).

  `Δgini_escape` (vs the no-demurrage baseline) is the residual redistribution gain after
  the escape; it must stay above the **0.05 design floor** the demurrage kernel was
  validated against.
- **Adoption sensitivity**: the same erosion at 25% / 50% / 100% of the top decile exiting.

**Reading it:** if the *entire* top decile wraps out at year 0, only the **5-year horizon**
keeps the residual redistribution gain above the 0.05 floor (Δgini_escape = 0.0796). Every
shorter horizon lets a total exit collapse the gain below the floor — the escape is too
cheap. Note the pool-revenue column: even at 5yr the one-shot escape (96,415 BTH) captures
only ~55% of what a decade of holding-and-paying would have (175,855 BTH), which is why the
escape still erodes the gain somewhat even at the recommended horizon — the point is to keep
that erosion above the floor, not to eliminate it (ADR 0003 §4 accepts post-wrap escape as
a deliberate stance).

|---------|------------:|------------:|------------:|

**Reading it:** the floor only binds under *heavy* adoption. At 25% or 50% of the top decile

exits that the horizon has to do work, and there **5 years is the sole survivor**.

## 5. Recommendation

**Recommended: `SETTLEMENT_HORIZON_BLOCKS = 31_536_000` (5 years at the 5s reference block
time; `5 × BLOCKS_PER_YEAR`, `BLOCKS_PER_YEAR = 6_307_200`).**

Rationale against the two failure modes:

- **Not a cheap escape (guards the Gini gain).** 5 years is the only swept horizon that
  keeps the residual redistribution gain above the 0.05 design floor **even if the entire
  top decile wraps out at once** (Δgini_escape = 0.0796). It is robust across the full
  adoption range, not just the benign partial-adoption regime. Shorter horizons rely on the
  bet that most wealthy holders *won't* exit — a bet the mechanism should not have to make.
- **Not punitive (bridge stays usable).** At 5 years the wrap cost is 1.9%–9.5% of the
  settled coin depending on factor class — a one-time on-ramp fee comparable to a few years
  of the demurrage the holder would otherwise owe, and far below the ongoing cost of *not*
  wrapping (the HOLD-world pool column shows a decade of holding costs ~76% more than the
  one-shot 5yr settlement). A mid-tier wealthy cluster (~2–3x) pays only 1.9%–4%. This is a
  friction, as ADR 0003 intends, not a wall.
- **Soundness is horizon-independent.** The churn-invariance margin is 1.0000 at every
  horizon, so 5yr does not compromise the #831 "no strictly-dominant escape" gate.

**Defensible alternative — 2 years (`12_614_400` blocks)** — if the maintainer judges that a
simultaneous 100% exit of the top decile is not a realistic threat and wants a materially
cheaper on-ramp (whale wrap cost 3.80% vs 9.49%). 2yr passes the floor at every adoption
level *below* 100% and fails only the worst case. This trades worst-case robustness for
on-ramp accessibility. **5yr is the conservative default; 2yr is the accessibility-leaning
choice.** 1yr and below are not recommended — they leave too little margin (Δgini_escape ≤
0.0318 at full adoption) and lean entirely on adoption staying low.

### Sensitivity notes

- **Linear in horizon.** Because the charge is linear in elapsed, doubling the horizon
  doubles both the wrap cost and (roughly) the pool revenue captured on escape. There are no
  cliffs; the maintainer can interpolate between the swept points with confidence.
- **The floor binds only under heavy adoption.** The horizon is a worst-case guardrail. If
  real adoption is partial, the choice barely matters for Δgini and reduces to on-ramp
  pricing — which argues for the smallest horizon that survives the worst case (5yr).
- **Factor sensitivity.** Wrap cost scales with the coin's factor; the whale class (5.745x)
  is ~3x more expensive to wrap than a mid-tier cluster (1.939x) at the same horizon, which
  is the intended progressivity — the wealthiest pay the most to exit.
- **Not swept:** partial-value settlement, repeated wrap/unwrap cycles, and second-order
  effects of the escaped value re-entering as wBTH-denominated demand. These are out of
  scope for setting a single scalar and do not change the ordering of the horizons.

## 6. Shared vs. separate horizon with #925

**Recommendation: use ONE shared `SETTLEMENT_HORIZON_BLOCKS` for both escape routes.**

#834's investigation (verdict `docs/research/demurrage-background-reset-leak.md`, PR #931)
confirmed a symmetric on-chain escape: a wealthy holder can deflate a coin to background on
an *ordinary spend* and pay **zero** future demurrage. Its fix #925 prices that transition
with **the same capitalized-future-demurrage formula over a horizon of the same magnitude
class**. The two are structurally identical "permanently exit the demurrage regime" prices:

| | #831 (this issue) | #925 (background-reset leak) |
|---|---|---|
| Escape route | wrap wealthy → factor-1, wBTH out | spend wealthy → background output |
| What's priced | permanent exit from demurrage | permanent exit from demurrage |
| Charge formula | capitalized future demurrage over H | capitalized future demurrage over H |
| Fee sink | lottery pool | lottery pool |

Because the formula, the factor input, the rate, and the economic meaning are identical, the
calibration in §4 applies **verbatim** to #925 — the wrap-cost, churn-invariance, and
erosion curves would be the same, since the sim already exercises the shared kernel.

**Why shared, not separate:**

1. **Arbitrage.** If the two horizons differed, the cheaper door dominates: a wealthy holder
   would always exit via the lower-priced route, making the pricier one decorative. #925
   exists precisely because a free on-chain door made #831's paid door decorative; two
   *different* paid doors reintroduce the same asymmetry in weaker form.
2. **One monetary-policy dial.** "How much does it cost to permanently leave the demurrage
   regime?" is a single economic question. Splitting it into two constants doubles the
   surface the maintainer must keep consistent, with no upside — the sim shows the optimal
   value is driven by the same floor and the same formula for both.
3. **Auditability.** ADR 0003 and #925 both point at the same cluster-tag inheritance bound
   (#713). A single shared constant keeps the two consensus carve-outs provably symmetric,
   which is exactly the property #925's verdict asks for ("the fix should MIRROR #831").

**Concretely:** define `SETTLEMENT_HORIZON_BLOCKS` once (recommended `31_536_000`) and have
both the #831 settlement op and the #925 deflation-pricing rule read that single constant.
If a future need to diverge them arises, that should be its own ADR with its own evidence —
the default posture is one dial.

**#925-specific over-charge check (issue #950).** Because #925 auto-applies the downgrade
charge to *every* deflating spend (unlike #831's opt-in op), it carries a false-positive risk
this sweep does not measure: it could over-charge honest activity that looks like a downgrade.
That surface is quantified separately in
[`demurrage-downgrade-overcharge.md`](demurrage-downgrade-overcharge.md) (sim
`downgrade_overcharge_sweep`, seed `0xB07A_0925`). Headline: over a realistic honest agent mix
the honest false-positive rate is **0.0000% at every horizon** while the #834 exploit is charged
1.90%–9.49% of value — so the honest side imposes no additional constraint on the horizon choice
above. The one caveat is a wallet-layer decoy-selection note (do not put wealthy coins in a
background spender's ring), not a consensus change.

## 7. What this deliverable is (and isn't)

This is the **calibration simulation + recommendation**. The final `SETTLEMENT_HORIZON_BLOCKS`
constant is a maintainer ratification recorded in ADR 0003 (or a new ADR), and it is that
ratification — not this document — that unblocks #831 and #925 for implementation. The sim is
committed and seeded so the numbers regenerate on demand and can be re-run against different
population assumptions if the maintainer wants to probe the recommendation further.
